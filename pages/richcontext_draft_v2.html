Rich Context


GOAL


The goal of the Rich Context project is to create a new platform that enables the search and discovery of datasets.

The challenge that empirical researchers face - It is very difficult to find out, for a given dataset:

  1. Who has worked with the data before;
  2. What methods were used;
  3. What results were produced;
  4. and, Where those products can be found.

Addressing that lack of information consumes an enormous amount of time and energy for many social scientists. A platform that provided automated tools and services to improve search and discovery could greatly facilitate the process — often by passively capitalizing on the accumulated labor of one’s extended research community.

There now exists the scientific capacity to build such a platform, and interest from the scholarly community, whose participation and contribution to this project are vital, as this is necessarily a community-based project.




TECHNICAL APPROACH


Python Library: We've built a library richcontext-scholapi which provides API integrations for federating discovery services and metadata exchange across multiple scholarly infrastructure providers. We regularly expand and develop the library by integrating additional APIs.

Knowledge Graph: We're creating a knowledge graph knowledge graph of known links between datasets, research publications, researchers and experts, and projects within the social sciences. The knowledge graph is the source of all rich context metadata.

Competition: Using our knowledge graph, we created a corpus of known links between research publications and datasets. Using the corpus, we designed a competition inviting NLP researchers to develop models to automatically detect dataset usage from the text of research publications. The competition is open to the public and ongoing, with team progress is tracked on a leaderboard. Our competition makes use of open source tools like science-parse from Semantic Scholar.

Interaction between Competition and Rich Context Scope Paco Nathan, DerwenAI


Jupyter Extensions: We've collaborated with Project Jupyter to create extensions which treat datasets as top-level constructs. Features like the Data Registry, the Metadata Explorer, and Commenting enable users to register datasets within research projects; browse metadata on their datasets while they work; and comment and collaborate in real-time on shared projects and datasets. Ongoing work involves enriching our knowledge graph with information gleaned from comments, collaboration and computing within the Jupyter environment; and developing a reccomendation engine for datasets and code.

COMMUNITY ENGAGEMENT


Competition: We've collaborated with an active community of researchers working on models for automated dataset detection in research publications. In addition to building their own models, teams are contributing to shared tools for pdf retrieval and text extraction.

Human-In-The-Loop: We've partened with RePEc (Research Papers in Economics), a repository of economics research with a lively and engaged community of contributors. RePEc's unique interface enables authors to validate and contribute metadata on research. With a sample of RePEc's contributors, we are piloting a program where contributors will validate outputs from the machine learning models that arae developed during the competition.

Read more about this work on RePEc's blog: New initiative to help with discovery of dataset use in scholarly work, by Christian Zimmerman
